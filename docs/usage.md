# How To Use BudgetFlow

BudgetFlow is made for everyday money tracking without turning it into accounting homework. The idea is simple: add expenses, group them by place or category, and let the app show the bigger picture.

## 1. Add Your First Entry

On the main screen, open the new entry form. Choose the date, currency, place or expense category, amount, and add details, notes, or tags if you need them. One entry can contain several items, which is useful when one purchase includes multiple expenses.

Tags are important in BudgetFlow. If you add a tag like `rent`, `groceries`, `macbook`, or `credit`, the app can use that tag later to connect the expense with a budget category, savings goal, or other tracked area.

## 2. Review Expenses In The Table

The main screen shows the expense table. It is the quickest place to check where your money went during a day, week, or month. You can search entries, filter them by date or place, sort them, open details, edit them, or delete them.

## 3. Use The Stats

The stats tab shows which expenses repeat most often and which categories take the biggest part of the budget. This is useful when you want to do more than record purchases and actually understand your spending habits.

## 4. Plan The Budget

In the budget section, you can set income sources, mandatory expenses, and planned expenses. This makes it easier to see how much money is already reserved and how much is still available without surprises at the end of the month.

Budget categories work through tags. When you create or configure a budget category, attach the tag that should belong to it. After that, every expense added to the expense table with the same tag will automatically be counted in that budget category. For example, if the budget category uses the `groceries` tag, a table entry tagged `groceries` will appear in that category and affect its spending progress.

This is also how BudgetFlow can warn you when a category is close to its maximum: the app compares tagged expenses against the category limit.

## 5. Track Savings

Savings goals are built around jars. Create a goal, set a target amount if you want one, choose a currency, and add tags that should feed that goal automatically.

When an expense in the table has a tag that matches a savings goal, it appears in that goal's history automatically. You can still add savings entries manually, but tags are useful when you want the table to stay the main source of truth.

## 6. Track Debts

The debts section keeps both sides of debt in one place: what you owe and what others owe you. Add the amount, currency, due date, priority, and type of debt, then record payments as they happen. The list can be filtered by status, type, and direction, so overdue or partially paid debts are easier to notice.

Debt reminders are also part of the notification settings, so the app can remind you before a payment deadline.

## 7. Scan Receipts

If you have a receipt, open receipt scanning and upload a photo. The app will try to recognize the receipt data, then you can review it and use it for an expense entry.

Receipt scanning currently works only for Polish receipts. Other receipt formats may be added later.

## 8. Adjust Settings

In settings, you can choose the base currency, app language, and font size. The base currency is used for summaries when entries are made in different currencies.

You can also connect Telegram accounts and configure notification rules: budget limit warnings, confirmations after new entries, debt deadline reminders, the minimum gap between notifications, and how often debt reminders should repeat.

## 9. Telegram Bot

The Telegram bot is planned for the near future and should be treated as an upcoming feature, not the main finished workflow yet.

The planned bot will notify you in Telegram when spending in a category is getting close to its maximum, open BudgetFlow as a Telegram Web App, and allow quick operations such as creating, reading, updating, and deleting expenses, debts, savings goals, and budget items.
